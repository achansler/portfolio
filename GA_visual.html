General Assembly

Visual Design Testing

Role

Research, UI

Description

The General Assembly website is a storefront for the courses and classes. Despite its importance, the majority of the website had not been updated since 2015 which caused many problems, one of which is an outdated visual style. During usability testing to find general website pain points, I learned that the visual style impacted users much more than previously suspected causing us to prioritize testing a UI refresh.

Research

Usability Testing

During usability testing, I focused on the highest trafficked pages: the home page, the browse page, and a class application page.

Current Flow

Questions I Asked

In addition to giving the tester a scenario and a task to complete, I asked the following questions to uncover how the visual design of the website represented the General Assembly brand.

“What are your initial impressions of this website?”

“What are some words you would use to describe this website?”

“What does this company offer?”

“Do you find this company trustworthy? Why or why not?”

“What questions do you have about this company?”

Results

The website visual design did not align with the rest of the General Assembly brand. It looked old and outdated, and therefore the company was perceived as unprofessional.

“It can be very jarring moving from one section to another.”

“I don’t trust this at all. It looks old and everything changes suddenly.”

“I don’t like this… It doesn’t match any other page.”

Conclusions

Users perception of General Assembly was that it was untrustworthy because of the visual design of the website. This could lead to an impact on revenue, as users only want to take a class from a reputable source.

I decided to test a redesign of the pages reviewed in the usability test, changing nothing else aside from the visuals.

Final Designs

With the support of a UI Designer, we worked together to create the start of a simple, AA Accessible system that could easily scale to the rest of the website. I took inspiration from the white open spaces of our campuses, and removed many decorative elements from the page while relying heavily on photography of students in the classroom, as previous A/B testing indicated photography in the place of iconography increased conversion rate.
