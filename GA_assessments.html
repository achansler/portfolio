General Assembly

Assessments

Role

Research, UX, UI

Description

At General Assembly, students take assessments before they start an online course to provide them with curated learning content, as well as at the end of a course to demonstrate how much they learned. When first created the Assessments tool lived separately from the learning software but due to its success, there was a need to integrate the assessments into the learning software to create a consistent experience for users.

Wireframes

Assessment Shell

Assessments are timed experiences. The test taker must submit their answers before the timer ends. I learned from past research that test takers felt uncomfortable when they didn’t know how much time was left in an assessment, and when they couldn’t easily find a way to submit their work. I explored where these important elements should go, and how they should interact with the collapsing sidebar that’s used throughout the learning software.

Final Exploration

To ensure that test takers had access to the two most important elements throughout the assessment, I placed them in the top right corner of the page. The content of the sidebar breaks down the assessment by section, as well as question number so a user can track their progress.

Question Types

Assessment writers were writing content for 5 types of questions: multi-select, single-select, short answer, ordering, and matching. Multi-select, single-select, and short answer questions all have well established patterns, which I used to make the experience as intuitive as possible. Ordering and matching questions, however, are unique.

Ordering Questions

The goal of ordering questions is to put the content in the correct order. I explored different ways to do so, including draggable answers.

Matching Questions

The goal of matching questions is to match one statement with another. I explored draggable answers, but because of the length of the questions this interaction was difficult on a mobile device.

Assessment Navigation Usability Testing

The way in which a test taker navigates throughout the assessment was incredibly important. The test taker had to both feel comfortable moving forward after answering a question, know that they could skip a question, and know that they could go back to a previous question to change their answer. I tested 3 ways of navigating through the assessment against each other:

Prototype 1

The first experience was an autosave experience. As soon as a user answers a question, their answer is saved in the background. Completed questions were checked off in the sidebar. A back arrow was prominent to indicate that a user could go back to any question.

Results

23%

of testers were concerened their answers were not saved.

“I want to know that my work is actually saved.”

Prototype 2

The second experience consisted of button that read “Save”, to comfort test takers. The test taker would save their answer, and then use the arrow to continue to the next question. As in prototype 1, completed questions were checked off in the sidebar, and a back arrow indicated the user could go back if necessary.

Results

38%

of testers didn’t realize they needed to click a separate save button to save each answer.

“I don’t know what happened because I definitely answered that question. That makes me sad.”

“I have to click on both [buttons]? That’s an obnoxious next step.”

Prototype 3

The third experience combined the next arrow and the save button, to a single button which read “Save and Continue”. Completed questions were checked off in the sidebar.

Results

“I like that the save and the proceeding is in one option.”

“One less thing to think about - this is what I was hoping it would do.”

Conclusions

Although testers wanted to save their answers themselves, they had a very strong reaction to separate actions to save and continue to the next question. Due to these results, I went with prototype 3 as the final version. Testers understood that the sidebar could be used as a navigation tool to go back to previous questions.

I also learned that a gray check next to a completed question indicated that the tester had answered the question, while a green check indicated that the answer was correct. Because of this, the final version of the design has a gray check next to the completed questions in the sidebar.

Final Designs

I kept the interface very simple to limit distractions during the timed assessment. The sidebar UI is consistent with the rest of the learning software to ensure a consistent experience for the user.
