General Assembly

Browse Experience

Role

Research, UX Lead

Description

The General Assembly website is a storefront for all of the classes, workshops, and events. Website visitors should be able to find a class that fits their needs, and sign up for that class. However, after conducting usability testing I learned that the website does not allow users to understand the products and does not offer an intuitive experience for discovering products that will appeal to them.

Research

Usability Testing

To discover general pain points, I conducted usability testing on the current website. The task of the test was based on the most common website goal: finding a career changing course.

Current Flow

Results

Based on the test, it was clear that users did not understand the various product categories, why different classes were in different categories, and generally struggled to find a class that fit their needs.

5/6

users could not understand product categories within topics.

3/6

were unable to successfully choose a class that fit the given scenario.

“You have to really know what you’re looking for and what you want to do to be able to use this…”

Conclusions

The General Assembly website had a discoverability and findability issue that lead to two problems:

Revenue impact: if users can’t find the product they’re looking for quickly, they’re more likely to abandon the site and turn to competitors. Based on user feedback, many people had such a difficult time finding the right class through the website that they resorted to searching on Google.

Lack of credibility: GA courses are marketed as a premium product. Many of those products focus on user experience design. Having a subpar website experience impacted that credibility, which likely impacted class enrollment.

Understanding the Problem

The admissions team receives calls from prospective students to answer questions, talk about the classes, and sign students up for the right class. The recorded calls became a great resource for me and my team when getting specific about the discoverability problem. With help from a UX Designer, and UX Writer we listened to 50 recordings, with the goal of uncovering exact confusion points in the browsing journey.

Results

of callers had questions about what specific class to take within a topic.

of callers had questions class structure.

“How long are the classes typically?”

“Do you offer that on the weekends?”

Conclusions

Based on the questions that we heard asked, we concluded that despite General Assembly’s varied and complex product catalog the website did not provide visitors a way to:

(a) Clearly understand what types of products GA offers. There are 4 distinct product categories—Immersive bootcamps, Evening and 1-week courses, On Demand learning paths, and Workshops. Users did not understand what characterizes each category, and how they relate to one another.

(b) With that understanding, find and make an informed choice about which product category and which specific product is the right fit for their career goals, skill level, schedule, budget, and learning style.

Wireframes and Usability Testing

Based on our findings, we focused our efforts on updating the browse page to include a way to filter classes. The filters were based on the conversations that the admissions team had with prospective students.

For the first round of wireframes we tested a standard e-commerce inspired browsing experience alongside a quiz-like experience, since the quiz that was on the website was a highly visited page.

E-commerce inspired wireframes

Quiz inspired wireframes

Conclusions

During testing, we learned that the quiz-like experience made people feel like they were forced down a path, while the e-commerce inspired experience felt familiar and natural.

Refinements

Because of our findings, we iterated on the e-commerce inspired experience focusing both on how the filters should be presented, and ensuring that the language used in the filters was natural rather than industry jargon which was heavily used throughout the website.

Final Designs

Both the filters themselves and the language used in the filters mapped to the most common questions that prospective students asked the admissions team, as well as search result history.

Page Organization

The page is organized by category. Each category starts with a label, as well as an explanation of the end goal of the classes within that category. Each course card, which is linked to a product detail page, elevates the most important information for a prospective student to make a buying decision: the class level, if the class is instructor or self-led, and how long the class is in hours.
